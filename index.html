definition:
learning: a process through which experience cause a lasting change in behavior or metal process

forms of learning:

  1. habituation: learn not to respond to the not important stimulation. this exsit in all animals with nervous systems helping to focus on important stimulation.
  2. mere-exposure effects: people tend to have a preference for things merely because they had exposed more.
  3. behavioral learning: a connection between two stimuli.such as we assocciate a ccertain scent with a particular per who wears fragrance.
  4. classical conditioning: is a basic form of learning in which a stimulus that produces an innate reflex becomes associated with a previously neutral stimulus, which then acquires the power to elicit essentially the same response.就是这玩意，产生了-体验-这种玄而又玄的东西。

For combat personnel aboard ship, this sound became strongly associated with danger—a CS for emotional arousal. The persistent effect of this association was shown in a study conducted 15 years after the war.

Real-World Applications of Classical Conditioning：

  1. dissuade wild coyotes from attacking sheep: They did so by wrapping toxic lamb burgers in sheepskins and stashing them on sheep ranches: When roaming coyotes found and ate these meaty morsels, they became sick and—as predicted—developed a distaste for lamb meat. The result was a whopping 30 to 50 percent reduction in sheep attacks! So powerful was this aversion that, when captured and placed in a cage with a sheep, the coyotes would not get close to it. Some even vomited at the sight of a sheep (Garcia, 1990).

operant conditioning:(voluntary, sometimes even suffering and operant)
**core concept 4.2:**In operant conditioning, the consequences of behavior, such as rewards and punishments, influence the probability that the behavior will occur again.
concequence:
the most powerful influences on behavior are its consequences
*law of effect(趋利避害):*The idea was that an animal’s behavior leads to pleasant or unpleasant results that influence whether the animal will try those behaviors again.

*continuous reinforcement:*rewarding every correct response and ignoring the incorrect ones provide quick and clear feedback about which responses are desired. it is useful for shaping the complex new behavior.
*intermittent reinforcement:*the rewarding of some, but not all, correct responses.intermittent reinforcement is the most efficient way to maintain behaviors that have already been learned A big advantage of intermittent reinforcement is its resistance to extinction.because we cant know whether the reward is going to appear.

punishment:
positive punishment: application of an aversive stimulus
nagative punishment: removal of a reinforcer

When you do decide to use punishment, it should meet the following conditions:

  • Punishment should be swift
  • Punishment should be consistent
  • Punishment should be limited in duration and intensity
  • Punishment should clearly target the behavior and be a logical consequence of the behavior rather than an attack on character of the person (humiliation, sarcasm, or verbal abuse) or physical pain.
  • Punishment should be limited to the situation in which the response occurred.
  • Punishment should not give mixed messages to the punished person
  • The most effective punishment is usually negative punishment

checklist for modify operant behavior:(if you want to change someone’s behavior)

  1. positive reinforcement
  2. negative reinforcement
  3. extinction
  4. punishment

comparison:

classical conditioning operant conditioning
Behavior is controlled by stimuli that precede the response (by the CS and UCS). Behavior is controlled by consequences (rewards, punishments, and the like) that follow the response.
No reward or punishment is involved (although pleasant and aversive stimuli may be used). Often involves reward (reinforcement) or punishment.
Through conditioning, a new stimulus (the CS) comes to produce “old” (reflexive) behavior. Through conditioning, a new stimulus (a reinforcer) produces new behavior.
Extinction is produced by withholding the UCS. Extinction is produced by withholding reinforcement.
Learner is passive (responds reflexively): Responses are involuntary. That is, behavior is elicited by stimulation. Learner is active (operant behavior): Responses are voluntary. That is, behavior is emitted by the organism.

关于for，while，do..while的一些想法

1.背景

本人长期受for等循环语句的困扰，每次在写循环时都要在脑子里面推演比较长的一段时间。为了加快思考速度，我总结了一些想法，有可能是各位有更好的方法

2.想法

2.1 循环的通用模型

循环可以看作是对状态（S，可以代表此时的内存中的变量的值）的判断和转换
for循环：判断（$ S_0 $） $ \rightarrow $ 运行（可能伴随着状态变化$ S_0 \rightarrow S_1 $） $ \rightarrow $ 判断（$ S_1 $） $ \rightarrow \dots$
while循环：判断（$ S_0 $） $ \rightarrow $ 运行（可能伴随着状态变化$ S_0 \rightarrow S_1 $） $ \rightarrow $ 判断（$ S_1 $） $ \rightarrow \dots$
do…while循环： 运行（可能伴随着状态变化$ S_0 \rightarrow S_1 $） $ \rightarrow $ 判断 $ \rightarrow $ 运行（可能伴随着状态变化$ S_1 \rightarrow S_2 $） $ \rightarrow $ 判断 $ \rightarrow \dots$

结论：
$ （S_{end} 为跳出循环的条件 ）$
分析循环时，可以列出$ S_0,S_1,S_2,S_3\dots S_{end} $一系列的状态，而由于无论那个循环都是的运行一定接着判断且跳出循环时一定是判断时，所以，进入循环运行内部的状态只有 $ S_0,S_1,S_2,S_3\dots S_{end-1} $

使用：
示例一：将一个数组复制到另一端内存上(伪代码)

可以将old[i]指向第一个元素时的i = 0为$ S_0 $
而目标状态为old[i]指向old的最后一个元素的i = old.size()-1为需要进入循环中运行的状态$ S_{end-1} $
所以判断跳出的条件为$ S_{end} $对应i = old.size()

示例二: 复杂一些，两个数（当然也可以一个）。一个从数组开头，向后移动，一个在数组最后向前移动,让数组倒置，当两者相遇并交换后停止
$ S_{0} $ head = 0; bottom = vector.size() - 1;
$ S_{end-1} $ 为 bottom - head = ((vector.size() % 2)? 0:1)
$ S_{end} $ 为 bottom - head = ((vector.size() % 2)? 0:1) + 2

这个方法可能有点傻，但是还是很清晰的，至少不会把人绕晕。但是有一定的局限，就是状态之间的转换不一定能很明白的被人理解，比如$ S_{0} \rightarrow S_{1} \rightarrow S_{2} \rightarrow S_{1} \rightarrow S_{2} \rightarrow \dots$ 并且状态本身可能很复杂。
